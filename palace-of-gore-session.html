Session 3 — Descent into Avernus

Palace of Gore

An Adventure Through the Lair of Bitter Breath

The Palace of Gore squats at the confluence of two rivers of blood, its walls built from coagulated infernal gore, ossified demon bone, and hardened blood-rock — the same materials the Blood War has been churning out for millennia. It is not a place designed to impress. It is a place designed to contain and to bleed.

Bitter Breath — an amnizu-class devil of middling ambition and vicious cunning — wrested control of this former demon beachhead through a combination of contract manipulation, poisoning, and sheer bureaucratic malice. He keeps the palace running through a garrison of enslaved hobgoblins and a rotating cast of infernal and demonic muscle he neither trusts nor particularly controls.

Somewhere in the deepest chamber, inside a cage suspended over a pool of condensed soul-stuff, the Moonkite waits. After decades of imprisonment in the Nine Hells, it is not the creature it was. But it is still alive.

Act One: The River of Blood

"Follow the river of blood and the smell of rotting guts." — Bloodbath the Drinker of Gore (as recalled by Rasheem)

The river of blood — real blood, not metaphor, not symbol — runs dark red and thick as syrup through a carved ravine of black stone. Something moves in it. Not fish. The smell hits you a quarter-mile before you see the palace: copper and sulfur and rotting meat baked under the Avernian heat. Ahead, where two tributaries merge, a structure rises. Not built, exactly. Accreted. Like a scab that grew walls. The outer skin of the Palace of Gore is made from layers of coagulated gore, pale bone fused into archways, and stone the color of dried blood. Torches burn a sickly green-blue along the battlements. You can hear, faintly, screaming — though whether it is structural or inhabited you cannot yet tell.
Lore — Palace of Gore Construction
In the Blood War, demon fortresses in Avernus are assembled from available materials: coagulated blood hardens into a stone-like substance over decades of infernal pressure. Ossified demon bone — particularly from Type 1 and 2 demons — forms the primary structural skeleton. The infernal rock of Avernus, already saturated with residual evil, bonds to these materials through necrotic binding. The result is a building that is, in a biological sense, mostly meat — and which leaks accordingly.

The Blood Gate

The palace's main entrance is a mouth-shaped arch of fused bone, sealed by a curtain of falling blood. Two hobgoblin sentinels flank the arch, bored and sweating. This entrance is not recommended — the blood curtain acts as a detection ward for celestial creatures.

Blood Gate Ward
Any creature with the Celestial or Fey type that passes through the blood curtain triggers a shrieking alarm audible throughout the palace. Lulu (hollyphant) and Whiskerbright (imp — not celestial/fey, safe) and the Moonkite Cage Key (radiates faint celestial resonance) will each trigger the ward independently. The sentinels will sound a horn and Grozz's forces will be at full readiness when the party reaches the Throne Room.
Investigation DC 13 Inspecting the blood curtain reveals the faint shimmer of an abjuration ward woven into the falling blood. A character who succeeds understands it will trigger on celestial auras. On a 17+: they also identify that the ward anchor is a small skull embedded in the arch's keystone — destroying it (AC 10, 15 HP, fire immunity) deactivates the ward.

The side tunnel approach (see Drainage Tunnels) bypasses the Blood Gate entirely. This is the intended infiltration route and does not trigger the ward — though it has its own problems.

Smiler the Imp SOCIAL

Something drops from the rock face to your left — not falling, exactly. Settling. A creature roughly the size and shape of a very ugly cat, with leathery wings folded against a mottled green back, perches on a bone protrusion and watches you with eyes the color of fresh bruises. It produces a small card from somewhere and holds it up. The card says, in careful block letters: SMILER — INTEL & SUNDRIES. "You're trying to get in," it says. It is not asking.

Smiler is an imp who has achieved something rare in the Nine Hells: a comfortable freelance arrangement. He sells information to anyone passing through this stretch of Avernus and has standing arrangements with half the warlords in a fifty-mile radius. He is not loyal to Bitter Breath. He is loyal to his margin.

Smiler — Sample Dialogue
“You’re trying to get in.” — Opening line. Not a question. He watches for their reaction.
“Information has a shelf life, friends. Especially here. The longer you stand in the open, the shorter that shelf gets.” — Haggling. Tapping his card against the rock.
“It’s been singing, lately. Sad songs. The boss doesn’t like it.” — On the Moonkite. His voice drops; this is genuine.
“A profitable slaughter, then. I’ll be watching.” — If deal accepted. He vanishes (Invisibility, not teleport — he’s still there).
He produces another card. It reads: PRICES JUST WENT UP. “I don’t negotiate at knifepoint. Professional standard.” — If threatened. Price increases by 2 soul coins.
“Your funeral. I hear the screaming’s lovely this time of year.” — If refused. Vanishes. The party goes in blind.
What Smiler Knows
  1. The blood gate ward triggers on celestial and fey auras. The side tunnel entrance (the Drainage Tunnels) bypasses it, but it's guarded by "the leftovers" — demonic troops Bitter Breath couldn't billet elsewhere.
  2. Captain Grozz commands the hobgoblin garrison. He's been there four years. He hates the demon troops and tolerates Bitter Breath because he has no choice. "He might be convertible, if you had leverage."
  3. The "fancy thing in the back chamber" (the Moonkite) is in a suspension cage behind the throne. Bitter Breath keeps it as a trophy and a power source. "It's been singing, lately. Sad songs. The boss doesn't like it."
  4. Bitter Breath's one genuine vulnerability: his phylactery — the infernal contract binding him to this posting — is kept in the throne's left armrest. If it's destroyed during the fight, he loses his Legendary Resistance for the remainder of the encounter.

Roleplay Paths

Persuasion DC 14 / Deception DC 12 / Intimidation DC 18 Negotiate the price down by 1 soul coin OR add an item to the existing deal. Smiler responds well to professionalism and badly to threats (imps value self-preservation, but they're also prideful — threatening him earns a cold smile and a 2 soul coin price hike).

Path A — Accept the deal: Smiler provides the information, wishes them "a profitable slaughter," and vanishes. He uses Invisibility, not teleportation — he's watching from the rocks for the next hour, curious how this turns out.

Path B — Refuse the deal: "Your funeral," he says pleasantly, and disappears. The party goes in blind. The ward and Grozz intel are lost. The phylactery info is lost. Bitter Breath's Legendary Resistance is intact.

Path C — Attack Smiler: He Readies an Invisibility reaction and vanishes before the first attack lands (DC 18 Perception to track him by sound). He teleports to Bitter Breath and reports that three mortals with celestial companions are incoming. Bitter Breath activates the palace's lair actions one round earlier and positions himself defensively. Grozz's forces are at maximum readiness.

DM NOTE — Lulu and Whiskerbright: The Blood Gate ward is the main reason to use the tunnel approach. If the party plans to bring Lulu through the side tunnel instead, the Drainage Tunnels themselves have no celestial detection — but Lulu's Trumpet ability will alert everyone in the palace if she uses it. Whiskerbright, as an imp (fiend type), moves freely and is a useful scout.

Act Two: The Drainage Tunnels

The side entrance is a crack in the palace's foundation wall — barely wide enough for two people abreast — that opens into a carved passage. Carved is generous: it looks gnawed. The walls are fused bone, recognizable as bone because fingers still protrude from some sections, reaching outward as if still trying to escape. The floor is smooth from decades of ichor seepage. Green-blue torches burn in skull-mounted brackets every thirty feet, casting light that makes everything look slightly rotten. The air smells like a butcher's shop in summer.
Tunnel Dimensions: Passages are 5 ft wide, 8 ft tall. Where bone formations intrude, ceiling drops to 6 ft (Large creatures must squeeze — half speed, disadvantage on attack rolls). The Screaming Gallery (Encounter 2) opens to 30 ft wide, 25 ft ceiling.

Lighting: Dim light throughout from skull torches. Darkness check applies for Shadow Demons (they can Hide as a Bonus Action in dim light).

Ichor Slick: Black ichor seeps from wall fissures in marked areas (described per encounter). Creatures entering a slick square must succeed on a DC 12 Dexterity saving throw or fall prone.
Lulu — Companion Reactions
“This place is made of people. The walls are made of people, Aurora. I don’t — I can’t —” — Entering the Drainage Tunnels. She presses against the nearest PC.
“He’s not like the others. His eyes are sad. Not angry. Sad.” — On seeing Captain Grozz. Whispered to Drenwal.
“THAT’S the Moonkite? That’s — she’s hurt. She’s really hurt, Aurora.” — First sight of the Moonkite in the cage. Lulu’s trumpet quivers but does not sound.
“She’s going to be okay, right? She’s going to be okay.” — After the Moonkite is freed. Not a question — a demand addressed to no one.

Encounter 1 — Charnel Convergence HARD

The Entry Hall is a roughly oval chamber, 30 ft wide and 20 ft long, with a 15-ft ceiling. It was once used as a demon barracks. It still smells like one.

The passage opens into a wider chamber. The floor is covered in a thin film of black ichor that catches the torchlight and throws it back in oily rainbows. Across the room, a single figure stands guard at the far door: a bearded devil, its glaive planted point-down in the floor, watching you with the blank patience of something that has stood guard for a very long time. The ceiling is shadowed. The two blood pools near the left and right walls seem very still — unnaturally still, as if they are holding their breath.
Perception DC 15 — Spot the Barlgura A character who looks up and makes this check notices faint claw marks pressed into the ceiling bone — and then notices the ceiling itself is breathing. The Barlgura has used its Phantasmal Force on itself to appear as part of the ceiling texture. On a failed check, it gets a surprise round.
Insight DC 13 — Wrong Blood Pools The blood pools near the walls are darker than the floor ichor and have no ripple pattern despite the air currents. A character who succeeds senses a wrongness — something is in those pools, not floating on them. The Shadow Demons lose their surprise round if this check succeeds before initiative is rolled.

Tactics — Corrected per Monster Behavior

THE BARLGURA — "Same Play, Every Time"
CR 5. INT 7. This creature does not adapt. It has three moves and will use them in the same order every single encounter because it has learned that order works and lacks the intelligence to improvise. If the party survives round 3, they can predict its next action.

Round 1 (Surprise, if not detected): Drops from ceiling onto the highest-threat target (whoever has the most visible armor or casting focus). Uses Phantasmal Force immediately — the illusory hazard should be something physically restraining: a pit of acid, a crushing stone wall, or a swarm of insects. The target must use their action to examine or resist it.
Round 2: Casts Entangle (centered on the cluster of remaining characters). This is area denial, not targeted — it catches allies of the Barlgura too, but the Barlgura doesn't care because INT 7.
Round 3+: Closes to melee with Reckless Attack, Multiattack (Bite + 2 Fists). It will fight until dead. It does not flee. It does not surrender. It does not change the plan.
SHADOW DEMONS — "Predators of the Dim"
CR 4 ×2. These are not mindless — they have WIS 13, INT 14. They are calculating hunters that understand the value of patience and self-preservation.

Opening: Both shadow demons use their Bonus Action (Shadow Stealth) to Hide in their respective blood pools before initiative. They have advantage on their first attack from concealment.
Targeting: They identify the weakest target — lowest visible HP, least armor, no shield. In this party, that is likely Aurora or Asimov depending on positioning. They do not attack Drenwal (Light Cleric) unless forced; radiant damage is their vulnerability.
Each turn: Attack from adjacent to target with advantage (Hidden), then immediately retreat through the nearest wall or floor (Incorporeal Movement). They cannot be cornered. They cannot be grappled. They laugh at Hold Person.
Self-Preservation Trigger: If a shadow demon takes any radiant damage, or is reduced below half HP while outnumbered, it sinks into the floor and does not return this combat. It is not destroyed — it will reappear elsewhere in the palace unless specifically hunted. Track the HP of each shadow demon.
BEARDED DEVIL — "The Wound That Keeps Taking"
CR 3. This creature will never retreat. It is literally incapable of choosing self-preservation over its infernal compulsion to hold the post it was assigned. It knows this about itself and weaponizes it — it fights with the confidence of something that cannot be scared away.

Priority: Get a grapple with the Beard attack on the first round. The Beard is not the main damage source — the Infernal Wound passive is. Once a target is grappled, it takes 5 ongoing necrotic damage per turn and cannot escape without a DC 13 Strength check (ending the grapple) or magical healing (which also ends the wound).
With grapple established: Switches to the Glaive (10-ft reach) to attack the other party members, or targets a second character with another Beard attack to spread wounds.
Goal: Have two active Infernal Wounds running simultaneously. With three party members, this alone is 10 damage per round the party cannot easily stop.

Encounter Outcome

If the party flees back into the tunnel, the Barlgura does not pursue beyond the chamber (it doesn't fit the narrow passage easily). The Bearded Devil will not leave its post. The Shadow Demons will follow for one tunnel segment then retreat. Fleeing will trigger a Patrol roll (1d4) immediately.

Encounter 2 — Screaming Gallery DEADLY

The Screaming Gallery is the palace's former ritual chamber, a vaulted space 30 ft wide, 50 ft long, and 25 ft tall with a gallery ledge running both long walls at 15 ft height. A shattered obsidian altar stands at the far end. The bone devil that commands this section has made it into a watchtower — elevation, sightlines, and a sound-based alarm system.

The tunnel disgorges you into a vast chamber that feels, for a moment, like breathing space — and then you look up. The bone devil is perched on the far gallery ledge thirty feet in the air, looking down at you with the patient consideration of something that has no reason to hurry. Below it, suspended in the air like a burning coal, something skull-shaped bobs and rotates slowly, trailing purple flame. On the near gallery ledges — left and right, fifteen feet up — two spined devils crouch in the shadows, wings half-spread, watching. Then, without warning, the altar at the far end begins to scream.

Stealth Bypass Option

There is a lower passage — a maintenance crawl, 3 ft wide — that bypasses the Screaming Gallery entirely. Whiskerbright can spot it with a DC 11 Perception check while scouting. Using it requires the following:

Group Stealth DC 14 — Crawl Bypass All party members (and Lulu, if present) must succeed. Advantage if Whiskerbright has scouted the passage. On success: The party reaches the antechamber without combat and without alerting anyone. On failure by any one character: The noise carries — the bone devil sends the two spined devils to investigate, arriving in 2 rounds (treat as a partial encounter at half strength). The bone devil itself remains in position.
Soul-Shriek Altar
The shattered obsidian altar at the chamber's far end contains a bound soul that screams continuously. The sound is a psychic resonance hazard, not physical noise. At the start of each creature's turn while in this chamber, they must make a DC 13 Wisdom saving throw or take 2d4 psychic damage and have disadvantage on Concentration checks until the start of their next turn.

Silencing the altar: Requires a DC 16 Religion check (action) to identify the binding glyph, followed by a melee attack on the altar (AC 10, HP 20, immune to fire and lightning). When the altar is destroyed, the screaming soul is released (DC 14 Arcana or Religion to safely release vs. scatter — scattered soul becomes a minor hostile haunt for 1d4 rounds). Destroying the altar causes the bone devil to descend and engage directly rather than commanding from height — this may be to the party's benefit.

Tactics

BONE DEVIL — "The Commander Who Stays Above It All"
CR 9. This creature is intelligent enough to know its best position is never the ground. It opens combat at maximum height (25 ft), uses its Fear Aura to force saves, and directs the Spined Devils by telepathy while its sting targets anyone who's concentrating on a spell.

Round 1: Fear Aura (DC 13 Wisdom — all within 30 ft). Then uses Sting on whoever just cast a concentration spell (targeting Aurora's Warlock slots).
Round 2+: Multiattack (2 Claws + Sting) while hovering 15+ ft off the ground. Repositions every round to stay out of melee range.
Self-Preservation: Unlike bearded devils, bone devils have high enough INT (13) to value their own survival. If reduced below 35 HP (25%), it will attempt to flee through the ceiling gap to the palace exterior. Track carefully.
On altar destruction: Bone devil descends in outrage (treats the altar as sacred despite it hurting everyone). Now in melee range. This is a trade — harder fight, no more Wisdom saves.
FLAMESKULL — "Kill the Priest First"
CR 4. Undead. Does not retreat. Cannot retreat — the compulsion that animates it also binds it to this location. It knows this and fights accordingly: maximum aggression, zero self-preservation instinct.

Round 1: Fireball centered on the tightest cluster of party members (save DC 13, 8d6 fire). This is the opener every time, even if it has a better option — it's the most spectacular thing it knows how to do.
Round 2+: Two Fire Rays per turn, targeting Drenwal (Light Cleric) specifically. Flameskulls universally prioritize divine casters — they recognize healing as the greatest threat to their side.
Reaction: Shield (+5 AC until start of next turn) when hit by a spell attack roll of 13–17. It will not use this against physical attacks.
Rejuvenation: If not doused with holy water immediately upon destruction, it reforms in 1 hour at full HP. Remind Drenwal.
SPINED DEVILS — "Reluctant Artillery"
CR 2 ×2. These creatures are cowards operating under orders. Left to their own preference, they would not fight at all. Their entire tactical approach is designed around never taking a hit.

Every turn: Stay at range. Approach to 20 ft for Spine Volley (Multiattack: 2 spines, range 20/80). Retreat 40 ft using Flyby (no opportunity attacks). Never closer than 15 ft unless cornered.
If cornered or targeted by ranged attacks: They break formation and scatter to ceiling corners. At ceiling height (25 ft), they are a secondary problem — spines still rain down, but with disadvantage from extreme range.
If the bone devil is killed: Both spined devils use their next turn to flee — they have zero loyalty and will report back to Bitter Breath if they escape. If this happens, Bitter Breath knows the party is coming and Grozz gets a warning.

Captain Grozz SOCIAL

The antechamber before the throne room is the only clean space in the palace. The floors are swept. The torches are evenly spaced. The hobgoblins standing at attention along the walls are in matching armor, their weapons oiled and presented correctly. Captain Grozz stands at the center, arms folded, watching the entrance with the particular expression of someone who has been expecting trouble and is mildly disappointed it finally arrived. He is built like a door. He has the eyes of a siege commander three wars past when any of this was fun. "You are not Bitter Breath's guests," he says. "Which means you are either a mistake or an opportunity. Which is it?"
Lore — Grozz's Blood Oath (Grok Research)
Grozz's garrison is bound to Bitter Breath through a formalized "blood oath of conquest" — a common arrangement between infernal commanders and mortal warbands seeking protection in the Blood War. The oath is real and magically enforced: Grozz cannot directly raise a hand against Bitter Breath without the oath being broken, which would result in painful divine punishment (3d6 psychic damage per day until released or dead). However, the oath has a significant limitation: it specifies direct violence. Grozz can choose not to defend Bitter Breath. He can choose to leave the antechamber. He can give the party information. These are not violations. They are omissions.

Internally, Grozz is conflicted. Serving under a demon's associate violates every principle of hobgoblin military culture — they revere order, discipline, and hierarchy, all things demons actively corrode. He has maintained strict military discipline in his garrison specifically to resist the chaos infecting the palace. He is looking for an honorable exit, even if he would not admit this under torture.
Captain Grozz — Sample Dialogue
“You are not Bitter Breath’s guests. Which means you are either a mistake or an opportunity. Which is it?” — Opening. Arms folded. Voice flat.
“I swore an oath. A bad oath, sworn in ignorance, to a creature that does not deserve the loyalty it purchased. But I swore it.” — If asked about his loyalty. He does not break eye contact.
“You found Kral.” A pause. Something shifts behind his eyes. “He was a good soldier. The fact that he survived says something about you.” — If Kral was freed first. Persuasion DC drops to 12.
“I will not raise my sword for that creature. But I will not raise it against my oath, either. You understand the distinction.” — Standing down. He steps aside. His guards follow.
“Forty feet wide. Thirty-foot ceiling. Blood pool in the center, balconies on the flanks. He sits on that throne like he built it. He didn’t.” — Giving the throne room layout. Precise, military briefing tone.
“We are soldiers, not merchants.” — If bribed. The guards behind him visibly react.

Act Three: Bitter Breath BOSS

Full stat block, mythic phase mechanics, secondary objectives, and per-phase lair action tables are in the companion document: bitter-breath-boss-encounter.md. This section provides the session-table reference cards and interactive trackers.
The throne room of the Palace of Gore is wrong in the way a painting of a room is wrong — the proportions are off, the shadows fall incorrectly, and something behind your eyes says: leave. The throne is made from a single fused mass of bone and coagulated blood-stone, twenty feet tall, carved with faces that are either screaming or sleeping — at this distance, it is difficult to say which. Bitter Breath sits on it with the posture of a middle manager who has been promoted beyond his comfort zone and has compensated by becoming very, very cruel. He is watching you with amber eyes that don't move. From somewhere behind the throne, something pulses — soft light, silvery-gold, like moonlight remembering what it was — and then a voice, thin and fractured, says: Aurora.
Bitter Breath — Sample Dialogue
“Ah. The Moonkite’s friends. I was beginning to think she’d made you up.” — Opening. He does not stand. He does not need to.
“You understand that you are trespassing in an official military installation? I have the paperwork. Somewhere.” — Phase 1 taunt. Bureaucratic malice. He is not joking.
“Do you hear that? That sound? That’s your patron learning what despair tastes like. I’ve been teaching her for forty-seven years. She’s a slow learner.” — Phase 2 (The Siphon). Directed at Aurora.
“I have had this posting for forty-seven years. You are the fourth group to try this. Do you know what happened to the other three? Neither does anyone else.” — Phase 3 (Stolen Starlight). Cold, final.
“This — this is a temporary setback. I have a performance review in — the contract specifies —” — Near death. The bureaucrat crumbles before the monster does.
“She will never fly again. You can open that cage and she will walk out and she will never be what she was. I made sure of that. That was my actual job.” — Last taunt at Aurora, below 30 HP. Whether true or not is left to the DM.

Act Four: Aftermath

The Moonkite's State

When the cage opens — when the key Aurora has carried all this way finally turns — what emerges is not what you imagined. It is still beautiful. That's the worst part. It moves like moonlight on water, all liquid silver and faint luminescence, the wings still shaped correctly, the form still recognizably equine and celestial and wrong for this place. But it is smaller than it should be. And it lands wrong — not floating, not hovering, but landing, weight on four legs that tremble slightly with the effort. It looks at Aurora. Its eyes are still silver, but the silver is thin, like ice over dark water, and underneath you can see something exhausted and very afraid. It says: I knew you would come. I stopped believing it, but I knew.
The Moonkite — Telepathic Fragments
Aurora. — First word. Thin and fractured. Heard only by Aurora, upon entering the throne room.

He tried to teach me despair. I learned patience instead. It looks similar from the outside. — If asked about her captivity. A flash of the creature she used to be.
Lore — A Celestial Fey in the Nine Hells (Research)
A creature of celestial and fey nature imprisoned in the Nine Hells for decades experiences progressive deterioration across multiple dimensions. Physically: sustained exposure to infernal energies weakens the body's celestial regeneration, reducing maximum HP and speed by approximately 30% until the creature can rest in a non-infernal plane. Psychologically: the absolute absence of hope — the defining quality of Hell — creates a form of alignment drift. The Moonkite has not become evil, but it has learned despair in ways that will require months of recovery. Its communication has grown twisted by proximity to infernal thought-patterns — it sometimes speaks in riddles or fragments, not because it cannot say the truth, but because it has spent decades in an environment where truth is always transactional. Magically: its connection to its patron (Aurora's archfey link) has been suppressed but not severed. Upon contact, it will feel the link reopen — and it will be overwhelming.

Moonbow Fusion — Immediate Scene

When the Moonkite touches Aurora's hand, the Wand of Celestial Warding and Aurora's bow undergo the Moonbow fusion spontaneously — see the mechanics below. This does not require a ritual. The Moonkite does not perform it intentionally. It is a side effect of the restored connection, the channeled celestial power flowing through Aurora's attunement items and crystallizing into a new form.
