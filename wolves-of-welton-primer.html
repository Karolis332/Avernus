Design Thesis

The hook is "kill the wolves." The truth is "the wolves are people now."

This oneshot lives or dies on one reveal: the wolves talk. Everything before it is sheep-rustling investigation; everything after it is a negotiated peace under threat of a civil war inside the wolf pack itself.

Three things make it work at the table:
  1. The bounty is suspiciously generous (800 gp for a wolf cull). Hint it loud. Suspicious players investigate; greedy players still get the moral reveal in the cave.
  2. Featherock is the keystone clue. A halfling shepherd, broken, whispering "the wolves were talking." If players skip him, the cave reveal lands cold. If they believe him, they walk in with their swords lowered.
  3. Flame vs Bolt is the real boss fight. Two alpha wolves, one wants peace, one wants slaughter. The party picks which one wins by who they kill, who they spare, and who they talk to.
Win state Wolves dealt with (peace or kill) AND the council pays AND the party can live with what they did. Lying to the council is on the table — Father Merriksonn rolls Insight +5 against Deception.

DM-Only Background

The Truth About the Wolves (do not read aloud)

Three months ago, the village sorcerer Alexi Merriksonn — wielder of Wild Magic — was patrolling the moors and stumbled into a normal wolf pack. He tried to drive them off, rolled a Wild Magic Surge, and was vaporized. The surge dumped his intelligence, language, and a sliver of his elemental power into the surviving wolves.

The pack woke up sapient. They learned Common in days. They built a fence. They drew on cave walls. They drove off the local Owlbear and took its den. They stole sheep — not to slaughter, but to breed. They're trying to live.

The alpha pair, Flame and Bolt, got the most magic — fire breath and lightning breath, respectively. They disagree on what to do next:

  • FLAME Chaotic neutral. Kill humans, terrorize them out of the woods, claim the land.
  • BOLT Lawful neutral. Negotiate or retreat, raise pups, never be hunted again.

Within weeks they will kill each other if the party doesn't intervene.

Nobody in Welton knows any of this. The village thinks it's a normal pack acting weird.

Scene 1 · ~30 min · Outdoor combat

1 · The Welton Wolfpack

Cold Open (read aloud)

The road from the high passes drops down through chalk hills and patchy woods, and below you sits Welton — white-walled houses, smoke from a dozen chimneys, sheep dotting the green like spilled flour. Easy country. Quiet country. Three hundred feet ahead, the road bends between a rock outcrop and the edge of a mossy wood. Two men in shepherd's cloaks are pushing a small flock through the gap, four sheepdogs working the flanks. Then the dogs go rigid. Ears up. Both of them barking at once. The shepherds reach for clubs at their belts — and from both sides of the road, grey shapes erupt out of the brush. Eight wolves. Moving like a single thing.

Roll initiative. Players are 150 ft from the action.

Encounter

8 Wolves vs. 2 Shepherds (Commoner, AC 10, HP 4, club +2 / 1d4) + 4 Sheepdogs (Mastiff) + ~12 sheep.

Wolf Tactics — Critical

The wolves are NOT trying to kill. They are stealing sheep.

  • 4 wolves harass shepherds — bite at legs/arms, no kill blows. Distraction.
  • 4 wolves grab sheep by scruff and bolt west at full speed (50 ft/round).
  • A wolf at >50% HP loss disengages and runs.
  • If 3 wolves drop, the rest break contact and flee west, leaving stolen sheep if needed.
  • No wolf fights to the death here.

Aftermath

If party intervened: shepherds Hennick and Toll are grateful. Walk back to Welton with them, seeded rumors on the way. No reward — poor farmhands.

If party watched: shepherds lose 6–8 sheep, are wounded, beg help to walk back. No hostility.

XP: 400 (8 × 50). Award full even if wolves fled.

Featherock's Room — KEY SCENE

Critical clue scene If players skip Featherock, the den reveal lands cold. If they believe him, they walk into Scene 5 ready to parley.

Willen Featherock, halfling shepherd. Badly wounded weeks ago in the failed posse. White-faced, sweating, bandaged left arm and right leg. Father Merriksonn attends him daily.

Scared. Will not talk freely. Opens up only if the party promises to believe him.

The Story (deliver in fragments)

  1. He was bitten, then trampled by his fleeing companions during the posse ambush.
  2. Drifted in and out of consciousness.
  3. He remembers gruff voices arguing in the darkness and a powerful grip on his ankle.
  4. If pressed gently: "They were talking. The wolves were talking. They were arguing whether to eat me."
  5. He was dumped on the Westly farm doorstep at dawn — alive, deliberately.
  6. Breaks down. Refuses to say more.
Recovery If the wolves' intelligence becomes publicly known, Featherock recovers within days. He'll come find the party to thank them.

Atmosphere (read aloud)

The woods press in tight. Moss-thick trunks, knee-high ferns, the smell of wet earth. Every few minutes something small bolts through the undergrowth — squirrels, rabbits, you can't tell. Once, far off to the west, a single wolf howls. Then nothing. Just the rustle.
Scene 4 · OPTIONAL · ~25 min · Combat filler

4 · The Owlbear's Picnic

Cut this scene if you're tight on time. It foreshadows that wolves drove an Owlbear out of its den.

The Encounter

After ~2h in the woods, the party hears snapping branches. Unless they hide (DEX Stealth DC 15), an Owlbear (modified, see statblock below) crashes into them.

  • Max HP 40 (reduced from 59) — bleeding for weeks
  • Limping, gaunt, half-healed bite wounds clotted with old black blood
  • Scorch marks in the fur (Flame's work)
  • Will not retreat. Starving. Charges easiest target. Fights to 0 HP.

Clues After the Kill

  • WIS (Nature) DC 12: wounds are 2+ weeks old, caused by sharp jaws
  • WIS (Nature) DC 10 OR proficiency: wolves do not pick fights with owlbears
  • If daytime: owlbears are nocturnal — this one was driven from its lair
  • Scorch marks: WIS (Survival) DC 12 — magical heat, not natural fire

XP: 700 (reduced HP makes effective CR ~2.5)

Scene 5 · ~60 min · THE REVEAL

5 · The Wolves' Den

Approach

Cave entrance ~10ft × 10ft in a low grey cliff. Smoke rises from a second hole ~30ft back on the hill above (chimney to the eastern sub-chamber).

Tripwire perimeter at the treeline. WIS Perception DC 13 to spot. Triggers bone noisemakers.

What the Party Sees from the Treeline (read aloud)

Through the trees you see a hollow opening before the cliff. Six wolves. Some lounging by an outdoor fire. Two of them are working on what looks like a wooden fence — with their teeth. One is tearing apart a sheep carcass with surgical care, like it's portioning out meals. Many of them wear strips of bright cloth or beadwork around limbs and necks. The scene is eerily peaceful. Eerily organized. Not a wolf den. A settlement.
Pause here. Let the players notice. Let it land.

The Back Door (Chimney Hole)

The smoke hole on the hill, ~30ft from the cave entrance, opens 15ft above the eastern sub-chamber floor — directly above the meeting-room fire. Big enough for a Small creature (halfling, gnome) or a Medium creature with effort + grappling.

Eavesdroppers will hear Flame and Bolt arguing:

Flame (snarled, hot): "They came armed. They came as hunters. We are weak as long as they think we are animals. Burn the next village. Make them flee. Take the land."
Bolt (low, thunder-soft): "And every season after, more hunters. More fire-throwers like the Lightbringer. We did not become this to die in a glorious last stand. We disappear. We go deep. The pups grow up free."
Flame: "The pups grow up starving if we crawl."

Continue ad-lib until the party acts. The argument lasts 5–10 minutes in-fiction.

Triggering Combat

The wolves panic-bark if: noisemakers trip, stealth checks fail, party walks into the hollow openly. One wolf shouts "HUNTERS!" in growling, halting Common.

All 6 outer wolves attack. After 1 full round, Flame & Bolt emerge from the cave with 4 more armored wolves (+1 AC from leather scraps).

Combat Tactics
  • All wolves intelligent. Aggressive Pack Tactics use.
  • Speed 50ft to outflank squishy casters.
  • Bolt uses Lightning Breath in lines that never catch allies.
  • Flame uses Fire Breath in cones, does not care if 1–2 wolves are caught — trades them for a full party blast.
  • Bite + DC 13 STR save knocks targets prone, then dogpile.

★ THE PEACE TRIGGER

When to trigger

After ANY ONE of these occurs, Bolt roars (thunder-shake the room) and bellows in Common:

Bolt: "HOLD! This fight serves none of us. Speak — or we all die for nothing."
  1. 4 wolves are killed
  2. Flame OR Bolt drops below half HP
  3. Any PC drops to 0 HP

Track this with the tracker above. When any condition is met, the tracker glows.

The party can ignore him. If they do, the wolves fight to the death. If they listen — Scene 6.

Scene 6 · ~30 min · Resolution + climax

6 · Settling the Scores

Branch A — Total War

Party refuses or fails peace. Wolves fight to the death.

  • Nursery wolves beg for pups' lives. Spare pups → surviving wolves vanish forever.
  • Threaten pups → guards fight to the death.
  • Contract fulfilled. Council pays 800 gp.
  • Featherock does not recover. Lives, but haunted.

Branch B — Negotiated Peace

Bolt explains everything:

  • Pack was normal until they brought down "the Lightbringer" (Alexi Merriksonn).
  • Flash of light. They woke up thinking.
  • They hoard food so pups don't starve, elderly aren't abandoned.
  • They drove off the Owlbear.
  • Bolt hates Welton's people but is not Flame — he wants out, not war.
  • His offer: woods + uninhabited western moors → the pack. No raids. Pack kills any other predator threatening Welton. A border.

★ THE FLAME BETRAYAL

Climax — Do not telegraph

The instant it becomes clear Bolt is striking a deal, Flame attacks Bolt from behind.

  • She bites him for massive damage (drop Bolt to ~1/4 HP, take him out of the fight)
  • She screams "COWARD!" in Common, eyes blazing
  • All armored wolves rally to her in a final desperate attack
  • Unarmored wolves fall back in fear and confusion — they don't know who to follow

This is the real boss fight. Flame + 4 armored wolves vs the party, Bolt bleeding on the cave floor.

If party wins AND stabilizes Bolt (DC 10 WIS Medicine or any healing): peace holds. Bolt becomes new alpha.

If Bolt dies in the chaos: peace dies with him. Surviving wolves flee. Sequel seed: a wounded, leaderless intelligent pack vanishes into the wild.

  • Party kills Bolt, lets Flame live: Flame consolidates the pack, starts deliberate murder raids. The village burns inside a month.
  • Party negotiates, then betrays: Bolt's pack hunts them across the region. Pack tactics + 2 elemental alphas. The province becomes hostile.
  • Party tells the whole village the truth: Welton splits. Half want peace, half want extermination. A second sorcerer arrives within a year. The wolves are either allies or conquerors.
  • A PC dies to Flame's Fire Breath: Bolt offers, in genuine remorse, to negotiate a body return and concessions. He won't undo the death but he'll make it mean something.

Walking Into Welton

The road curves down out of the hills and the smell hits you first — wet wool, woodsmoke, and the sour green tang of a sheep pen that wants mucking out. The river runs slow on your right, brown with spring melt. White-painted houses crowd the high ground above it, their oak beams carved with twisting vines and stylized rams' horns. Welton at noon isn't bustling. It's crowded. There are too many people in the muddy main square for the time of year — farmhands sitting on barrels, women with hands wrapped around mugs that have gone cold, a knot of old men by the well not so much talking as muttering. Children chase a piglet between their legs. A dog barks once and lays down again. Nobody is looking at the road. They're all looking west.

Sensory Hooks to Drop In

  • The smell of damp wool, smoke, and turned earth
  • Bleating from a pen at twice normal capacity — sheep brought inside the village walls
  • Two children playing "wolves and shepherds" in the street; one starts crying when she's "caught"
  • A nervous mother snatching her son indoors as the party passes
  • Notice boards covered in fresh handwritten flyers: REWARD — WOLVES — see council

The Shepherd's Crook — Exterior

Three stories of black-stained oak under a steep slate roof. A weathered wooden sign hangs over the door — a shepherd's crook crossed over a tankard, the paint chipped pale. Smoke leans east from a chimney that runs the full height of the building. The shutters are open. From inside you can hear voices, but not laughter.

The Shepherd's Crook — Common Room

The door sticks. When you push through it, the room hushes for a half-beat — every head turns, every cup pauses — and then conversation picks back up at half its previous volume. The room is long, low-beamed, packed. Smoke layers under the rafters from a dozen long-stemmed clay pipes. The smell is wet wool drying by the fire, old beer, mutton stew, something herby — rosemary, maybe sage — and underneath it all the bitter dust of a hearth that's been burning continuously since autumn. Tables are full. Old shepherds in shapeless brown cloaks claim the corners, faces brown as boot-leather, talking through teeth that keep the pipe stem in place. Younger men and women — driven off their farms — share long benches by the wall, nursing one drink to last the day. A halfling woman knits in the chimney corner, watching everything. At the bar, a stout dwarven woman with grey-streaked black braids and forearms like firewood is pulling pints from a hand-cranked tap. She looks up. "You're new."

Leanor Slatebeard — Sample Dialogue

Friendly, sharp-eyed, knows everybody. Slight Dwarvish burr — drops the h off "have" and rolls r hard.

On arrival

Leanor: "Adventurers? Aye, you've a look of it. Drop your packs by the wall — nobody'll touch them under my roof, and if they do I'll know inside the hour. What're ye drinking?"

If they mention the wolves

Leanor (face changes; leans on the bar): "Then ye'll be wanting the back room. Council's in session and they've been at each other's throats for two hours. Free round when ye come out, mind. Banteth's making stew."

On Featherock

Leanor (voice drops): "Wee Willen Featherock. Upstairs, second on the left. Was a sweet lad, full of stories — now he's full of the wrong stories. Be gentle with him. He's not lying, whatever ye end up thinking he is. Now go on, before Tillus starts a fistfight in there."

On the bounty (if pressed)

Leanor: "Eight hundred gold. For wolves." (Pours your second pint without being asked.) "Aye. That's what I said."

On Father Merriksonn

Leanor: "Good man. Better priest than half the gold-collars I've seen pass through here. His brother going missing — that broke something in him, though he won't show it. Don't poke at it unless ye've a reason to."

The Council Room (when the party enters)

The back room is hot and close. A long oak table — scarred from a century of meetings — runs down the middle, eight high-backed chairs around it, seven filled. A pewter ewer sits in the center beside a bowl of soft cheese gone slightly oily in the heat. Three lit candles, one guttering. The window is shut. The air smells of pipe-smoke, sweat, beeswax, and the particular dry-paper smell of too many ledgers in one room. Two men are shouting. The halfling — Tillus Merrion, you'll learn — is half out of his chair, finger jabbing at the priest across from him. He's red in the face. His doublet is good wool, the buttons silver. "—will not — let — this village — go bankrupt over a problem we should have solved by — " The priest — older, white-haired, kindly face gone hard — cuts him off. "You will not let this village starve, Tillus, while you ship wheat to merchants who'll never set foot here. Sit DOWN." Tillus does not sit down. A fourth man — lean, weathered, a tuft of raw wool tied to the lapel of his vest — clears his throat softly. The room goes quiet. "There are people at the door."

Council Dialogue — Voices & Sample Lines

Tillus Merrion — Halfling, ledger-mayor

Speaks fast, clipped sentences, hands always in motion. Calls people by their last names. Hates being interrupted.

Tillus (interrupted): "Yes? Yes, who are — adventurers? Adventurers. Good. Sit, sit. We've been waiting for you. Whether we wanted to or not."
Tillus (pitching contract): "Eight hundred gold. Eight hundred. Half on agreement, half on the heads of the alphas — or whatever passes for them. Free lodging at the Crook. Free meals from Slatebeard's kitchen. Done in a week, you go home wealthy and we go back to working. Are we agreed?"
Tillus (party hesitates): "What — what do you want, a bonus? A cut of the spring wool harvest? Speak up, I've a freight contract due in Tarsweald in nine days and the road's not getting safer."
Tillus (told the wolves are intelligent): (Long silence. Then very quietly:) "Don't be ridiculous." (Pause.) "You're not joking. Are you joking."
Tillus (losing council vote): "This is — no. No, you cannot possibly be — Corel, you can't agree with this — Mella, listen to me —" (He realizes the room has turned against him. Sits down slowly. Doesn't speak again that night.)

Father Johan Merriksonn — Priest, brother of dead sorcerer

Speaks slow, warm, measured. Quotes scripture only when grieving. Looks each person in the eye.

Father Johan (greeting): "Welcome, friends. I'm sorry you find us shouting — the wolves have been hard on everyone's tempers." (Half-smile.) "I'm Johan. Father Johan, if titles matter to you. They don't to me. Please. Sit."
Father Johan (describing the situation): "Three months ago, we'd have called them wolves and meant it. Now —" (He stops, considers his words.) "Now we don't know what we're calling them. They take whole flocks. They open gates. A posse went out — good men, half of them my parishioners — and only six came back, two of those wounded. One of those... isn't himself anymore. Willen Featherock. Upstairs. You should speak with him."
Father Johan (on his brother): (Doesn't look at you when he answers.) "Alexi was — is — the best of us. The village never thanked him properly. He went into those woods six weeks ago and didn't come back, and the wolves started… changing… at the same hour. I don't know what that means. I think I do, and I don't want to."
Father Johan (party suggests peace): (Long silence. He closes his eyes. When he opens them, his voice is steady.) "If they killed my brother — and I think they did — then peace with them is the only thing his death could ever have been for. Make it real. I will speak for you in the council."
Father Johan (catches them lying): "Don't." (Quiet.) "Don't lie to me. Not about him. I will not tell the village. I will not break your contract. But you and I, we will not pretend, you and I. Is that clear."

Corel of the Fleecers — Master shepherd

Speaks rarely, in short sentences. Looks at the floor when thinking, at your eyes when answering. Knows the land better than anyone alive in Welton.

Corel (introducing himself): "Corel. Fleecers. Forty years on the hills west of here." (Pause.) "Lost twelve sheep last month. Pulled the rest in. If you mean to find the den, I'll walk you to the treeline."
Corel (offering to guide): "Past the treeline you're on your own. I'll not go further. Not because I'm afraid of wolves. Because I'm afraid of those wolves, and I want to come home to my wife."
Corel (on the peace question): (Looks at you a long time.) "Forty years I've killed wolves to keep my sheep. If these ones can talk — if they think — then I've maybe killed somebody's grandfather every winter, and didn't know it. We talk."

Mella Tinnsfoot — Halfling community rep

Mella: "I represent the halflings. We're a quarter of this village and twice the worry. If your peace deal means the woods are off-limits for foraging, my people starve faster than the humans do. Make me a guarantee on mushroom rights or I vote no."

Hanna Westly — Farmer rep (Featherock was dumped on her doorstep)

Hanna: "I just want my husband to sleep through the night. He hasn't, not since Featherock turned up on our doorstep." (Looks at her hands.) "If they'll leave us alone — I'll vote for whatever lets us sleep."

Willen Featherock's Room (extended)

Second door on the left, end of a narrow hall. Floorboards creak in a specific pattern under your weight, like the room is announcing you. The door is open a finger's-width. Inside, sunlight slants through one shutter, lit dust motes hanging in the still air. A halfling lies on his back in a quilted bed, his left arm and right leg bandaged thick with linen that's been changed recently — there's a small wooden bowl on the chair beside him with brownish water in it. A sprig of cut white flowers in a clay cup on the dresser. A wooden carved sheep on the bedside table, very old, the paint mostly worn off. Featherock turns his head when you come in. His face is the color of dirty paper. His eyes are too wide and don't track right. "Are you — Father Johan, he said someone was coming. Are you the ones who're going to find them?" His voice is dry. He swallows. "I have to tell you something. But you have to promise. You have to promise you'll believe me."

Featherock's Story (deliver in fragments)

Featherock: "I went out with the posse. Six weeks ago. Maybe seven. I lose track in here." (Looks at the ceiling.) "We had torches. The wolves came out of the dark and the next thing I knew I was on the ground and somebody was on my leg — someone, not something, you understand the difference?"
Featherock: "I heard them arguing. Arguing. In words. One voice was hot, angry, kept saying kill them, kill all of them. The other voice was — was lower. Slower. Said no, this one is small, this one is afraid, this one is not the threat. And then —" (He starts to cry, doesn't seem to notice.) "And then I felt teeth let go of my leg. And I went to sleep. And I woke up on the Westly farm, in the morning. With my wounds cleaned. Wolves don't clean wounds."
Featherock (if you tell him you believe him): "Oh." (Long pause. His face breaks. He covers it with his good hand.) "Oh, gods. Oh, thank you. Thank you."
Featherock (if you don't believe him): (Goes quiet. Turns his face to the wall.) "Father Johan said you would. He was wrong. Please go."

The Woods — Time-of-Day Flavor

Morning (mist still in the trees)

Wet leaves. Cold breath. The sun is somewhere behind the canopy and the whole forest is the color of weak tea. Spider-webs strung between branches show as cords of beaded glass. Far off — a hundred yards? Two hundred? — something the size of a deer moves and stops moving and you cannot tell which direction.

Midday (heat under the canopy)

The forest at noon is loud with insect sound. Sweat under your collar. The smell of crushed bracken under your boots. Every fifty yards there's a sun-shaft cutting down through the leaves and you walk through them like passing through rooms.

Dusk (the howl)

The shadows long, the air cooling fast, and from somewhere west — closer than it has any right to be — a single wolf howls. It's not the long sad howl of a tracking beast. It's two short notes and then a held one, like a signal. You wait for the answering howl. None comes. The forest gets very quiet.

Night (don't camp here)

Black like the inside of a coffin. Your eyes never adjust because there's no moon under the canopy. The night noises stop when you stop and start again when you move. You feel watched and you are not wrong.

The Hollow — First Glimpse (extended)

You ease through the last of the brush and the trees thin and there it is — the clifface ahead of you, grey limestone streaked black where rainwater has run down it for a hundred years. The cave entrance is a low arch, ten feet wide, propped open by a single weather-greyed beam wedged sideways under the lip. Smoke rises from above and behind it — a thin grey column from a hole in the rock thirty feet back, drifting east on the breeze. Below the cliff, in the hollow, six wolves. One lies near a small fire pit, eyes closed, head on its forepaws. Two are working a low wooden fence — broken branches lashed together with strips of leather — pushing a new piece into place with their muzzles, then biting and tugging the lashings tight. A third is butchering a sheep with surgical care, separating cuts with its teeth — not gorging, portioning, laying strips of meat on a flat stone to dry in the sun. The remaining two are simply lying in the long grass, watching everything. Sentries. Every one of them wears something. Strips of red cloth wrapped around a foreleg. A necklace of beads on a sinew cord. A bit of woven wool tied behind one ear. One of them — the butcher — has what looks like a small leather pouch slung across its chest on a knotted cord. None of them are looking at the cave. They're not on guard inwards. They're on guard outwards. They are protecting something inside. And every detail of the scene — the fence, the fire, the meat-drying stone, the ornaments, the patient organized work of it — every detail says one thing. Not a den. A home.

The Cave Paintings (read aloud when party reads them)

The paintings are crude — charcoal and white ash, the lines smudged from being painted by something with no fingers. But they tell a story, left to right, like a frieze. First panel: Four wolves. Just wolves. Stick-legged, sharp-eared, running through stylized trees. Second panel: A figure stands among the trees. Tall, two-legged, hands raised. Lines radiate from his hands — light, or magic, or fire. The wolves have stopped running. They are crouched. Third panel: A bright sunburst, drawn over and over in concentric circles, taking up half the wall. In the middle of it, the figure is gone — not killed, gone. A small smudge of white ash where his outline used to be. Fourth panel: The wolves stand on two legs. Or it's drawn that way — their backs are straighter, their heads held up, their forepaws spread like hands. Around them, faint lines like the ones that came from the figure's hands. Fifth panel, the largest: A circle of wolves around a fire. Pups in the center. Above them, drawn small in the corner, a single human figure — running away. A red ash handprint covers his face. Whether the print means enemy or do not kill is impossible to tell.

Flame — Voice & Sample Lines

Harsh, fast, clipped. Bites her own words. Uses Common like she's still learning the rhythm — wrong stresses, missing connecting words.

Flame (argument with Bolt, overheard): "Speak SOFT. Speak soft for the killers. They came with iron. They came with torches. They will come AGAIN. The pups will be ash, Bolt."
Flame (demanding): "You give them words. Words. I give them fire. Fire stops them. Words make them brave."
Flame (betrayal): (Bites Bolt without warning. His blood is on her teeth when she speaks.) "COWARD. You die for the pack now, brother. Die well. I make pack live."

Bolt — Voice & Sample Lines

Speaks low. Long pauses. Picks each word. Lengthens vowels when calm, cuts them short when angry. His voice rumbles — when he raises it, things vibrate.

Bolt (ceasefire): "HOLD." (The cave shakes. Dust falls from the roof.) "This fight serves no one. Not yours. Not ours. Speak — or we all die for nothing."
Bolt (explaining the change): "We were wolves. Then there was a man. He was bright. He raised his hands and he was — gone. And we were — new. I knew my name. I had never known my name. I had never had one. Now I do." (Pause.) "The taste of words is strange. Like meat that is too hot."
Bolt (negotiating): "We take the woods. We take the moors past the woods. We do not raid your sheep. We kill any other thing that comes for your sheep. We do not speak to your kind again. If you come into the deep woods, we cannot promise. Out of the deep woods — you are safe. Your pups are safe." (Slow.) "Is this a deal that a man can make?"
Bolt (dying, if it happens): (Voice losing its rumble, going almost human-soft.) "Tell the Lightbringer's brother that we did not eat his man. We took his thinking. It is in us now. It will... try to be... kind."

Father Merriksonn — If the Truth About Alexi Comes Out

He doesn't speak for a long time. Then he sits down, and he sits down hard, like his legs have given up. He puts his face in his hands. When he looks up, his eyes are wet but his voice is steady. "So he didn't die afraid. He died — making something. Of course he did. Of course that's how Alexi would go." (He laughs once, broken.) "Of course. Of course. I'll need a moment." Later, when he returns: "I will speak for them in council. For all of them. He would have wanted that. He would have insisted. Now. Tell me what they need. Tell me what they want. Tell me everything."

Epilogue Beats

If peace was struck

Three nights later, on the road out of Welton, you see a single wolf at the edge of a moonlit field. It watches you. It is too big to be a normal wolf. After a long moment, it raises one forepaw — slowly, deliberately, like a man tipping his hat — and then it is gone into the dark.

If pack killed, pups spared

A week later, in a tavern fifty miles east, a tinker tells you he saw something on the road at dawn — a half-dozen wolf pups, alone, walking in single file, eastward. One of them, he says, looked at him "like it had a question it couldn't ask." He laughs. He's drunk. You don't laugh.

If entire pack wiped out

Welton is grateful. Welton is generous. Welton offers a feast in your honor at the Shepherd's Crook, and Leanor pours the good wine she keeps in the back, and Tillus makes a speech that's almost warm. Father Merriksonn doesn't come.

If a PC died

Their body is brought back to Welton on Corel's wagon. Father Merriksonn performs the service. Half the village comes. Tillus pays out of his own purse for a stone marker — not the council's coin, his own — and stands at the graveside in the rain with his hat in his hand, and does not speak.
